Order Data Cleanup & Optimization in ERP (Perpack)

In this project, I simulated a real-world scenario I encountered during my time as a Database Administrator at a skincare retail company. The problem? SKU inconsistencies across multiple systems made it difficult to track product transactions properly — especially when identifying pending orders. Some systems still used old SKUs, some used new SKUs, and some used both. This caused chaos when analyzing transaction data or trying to match product movement. So I built a solution using a SKU mapping table that unified the SKU logic and helped me clean and align the data.

The Data Structure

I worked with three main tables:

  • products – the product catalog with all active SKUs
  • transactions – a list of sales and pending orders (some using old SKUs)
  • sku_mapping – the key to everything! It contains:
    • sku: can be either the old or new SKU
    • desc: explains whether the SKU is an "old" or "new" one
    • new_sku: the standardized SKU that we want everything to align with

My Role in the Process

As a DBA, i designed the flow so every incoming transaction (regardless of SKU version) could be translated into a standardized SKU, using the mapping table as the translator. Once all SKUs were normalized, I ran checks to find orders that were still in "pending" status but had valid, active SKUs in the product master. This helped other teams (like fulfillment and ops) take action quickly.

Why This Matters

A pending order might seem small, but when you're dealing with hundreds or thousands per day — and SKUs don't match — it's a nightmare.

This approach:

  • Prevented missing or misread orders
  • Improved data alignment between departments
  • Enabled faster decision-making (and cleaner dashboards!)

Takeaway

"You can't analyze what you can't align."

This project taught me that even small inconsistencies like SKU mismatches can ripple into huge operational delays. As a DBA, my job isn’t just keeping the database alive — it’s making sure the data inside it actually makes sense.
