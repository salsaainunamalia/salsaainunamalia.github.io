Project Details

This project presents the design of a streamlined data flow, database structure, and ERD for a system that enables employees to view and explore detailed information about company assets. It serves as the foundation for a reliable internal platform tailored to organizational needs.

Desc: Illustrates core entities and relationships for asset tracking, employee roles, location hierarchy, and user access. Supports end-to-end visibility, secure role-based control, and structured data flow across business units

Desc: Depicts interactions between Admin and Users. Admin manages data; Users access asset info and history. Includes include/extend relationships for process clarity..

Designing a Role-Based Login System for Asset Management

This project was initiated to address inefficiencies in the company’s asset management process. Previously, asset tracking and recording were done manually, which often led to errors, inconsistencies, and difficulties in locating or transferring assets. There was no proper record of asset transfers, making it harder for both IT and General Affairs (GA) to monitor equipment such as PCs, networks, desks, and chairs.

During the early stages, I faced several challenges due to a lack of coordination within my IT team. For more than a month, I did not receive clear direction or briefing from my leader or IT support, which caused delays. Additionally, my leader often emphasized only technical issues and “the simplest way out,” without considering user convenience. To ensure the system truly helps users, I decided to take initiative and consulted directly with the GA senior, who manages office assets daily. From these discussions, I identified the need for an Asset Transfer feature, which was not initially considered. I then integrated this feature into the system design so users could track asset movements more effectively.

This proactive approach helped shape a system that is not only technically functional but also user-friendly and aligned with real operational needs.

Entity Relationship Diagram (ERD) Overview

The ERD represents the database structure for the Asset Management System. It consists of multiple interconnected entities that handle assets, users, employees, locations, and asset transfers. Key points include:

  1. Core Asset Data
    • assets table stores asset details such as specifications, serial number, model, category, and status.
    • category_asset and sub_category_asset provide classification and categorization.
  2. Organizational Structure
    • employees, departments, and bisnis_unit represent the organizational hierarchy and relationships.
    • locations and buildings manage the physical placement of assets.
  3. Transactions & Tracking
    • asset_transfer records asset movement between employees or locations.
    • user_assets links assets to employees for tracking assignments.
  4. User & Role Management
    • users table manages login credentials, linked with roles for access control.

Together, this ERD ensures the system is capable of managing both static data (like categories and locations) and dynamic transactions (such as transfers and user assignments).

My Role in the Process

As a DBA, i designed the flow so every incoming transaction (regardless of SKU version) could be translated into a standardized SKU, using the mapping table as the translator. Once all SKUs were normalized, I ran checks to find orders that were still in "pending" status but had valid, active SKUs in the product master. This helped other teams (like fulfillment and ops) take action quickly.

Why This Matters

A pending order might seem small, but when you're dealing with hundreds or thousands per day — and SKUs don't match — it's a nightmare.

This approach:

  • Prevented missing or misread orders
  • Improved data alignment between departments
  • Enabled faster decision-making (and cleaner dashboards!)

Takeaway

"You can't analyze what you can't align."

This project taught me that even small inconsistencies like SKU mismatches can ripple into huge operational delays. As a DBA, my job isn’t just keeping the database alive — it’s making sure the data inside it actually makes sense.
